Section 1: Introduction to Suspension Systems

Duration: 30 minutes

What is a Suspension System?

The suspension system is a critical component that connects the vehicle body to its wheels. It serves multiple essential functions:

üõ°Ô∏è Comfort

Absorbs road shocks and vibrations to provide a smooth ride for passengers

üéØ Control

Maintains tire contact with the road for better handling and steering response

‚öñÔ∏è Stability

Keeps the vehicle stable during cornering, braking, and acceleration

üîß Load Support

Supports the vehicle weight and any additional cargo or passengers

Basic Suspension Principles

All suspension systems work on the same fundamental principles:

Component Function
Springs Store and release energy to absorb road impacts
Dampers (Shock Absorbers) Control spring movement and prevent bouncing
Links and Arms Connect wheels to the vehicle body and control wheel movement
Bushings and Joints Allow controlled movement while isolating vibrations
üî¥ Safety Note: Suspension components are under extreme load and pressure. Always follow manufacturer procedures and use proper safety equipment when working on suspension systems.

Shock Absorbers (Dampers)

Shock absorbers are essential for controlling spring oscillation. Without them, the vehicle would bounce continuously after hitting a bump.

How Dampers Work

Dampers contain hydraulic fluid that passes through small valves:

  • Compression: Fluid flows through valve as piston moves down
  • Rebound: Fluid flows back as piston moves up
  • Resistance: Creates damping force that slows spring movement
  • Heat: Energy converts to heat, which must be dissipated
‚ö†Ô∏è Wear Indicator: Leaking fluid from a shock absorber indicates seal failure and requires replacement. This is a common MOT failure point.

MacPherson Strut Assembly

The MacPherson strut is the most common front suspension type. Understanding its components is essential:

Component Location Function
Top Mount Upper end (body attachment) Allows rotation, isolates vibration
Bearing Plate Top of strut Permits steering rotation
Coil Spring Around shock body Supports vehicle weight
Dust Cover/Bump Stop Upper strut Protects rod, limits travel
Shock Absorber Central cylinder Controls spring movement
Lower Mount Bottom (wheel hub attachment) Connects to steering knuckle

Double Wishbone Suspension

The double wishbone (or double A-arm) suspension is another common design, often found on performance vehicles and rear suspensions:

Key Features

  • Upper Control Arm: A-shaped arm attached to upper chassis point
  • Lower Control Arm: A-shaped arm attached to lower chassis point
  • Separate Spring/Damper: Can be mounted separately or as coilover unit
  • Ball Joints: Top and bottom joints connect arms to wheel hub
  • Better Geometry: Allows precise control of wheel movement and camber changes

Section 4: Tools, Safety & Legislation

Duration: 25 minutes

üî¥ CRITICAL SAFETY INFORMATION

Suspension work involves serious hazards:

  • Compressed springs store enormous energy
  • Improper release can cause serious injury or death
  • Heavy components can fall when supports fail
  • Vehicle must be properly supported at all times

Personal Protective Equipment (PPE)

üë∑ Safety Glasses

MANDATORY

Protects eyes from flying debris, rust, and fluid splashes

üß§ Gloves

MANDATORY

üë¢ Safety Boots

MANDATORY

üëî Overalls

REQUIRED

Protects body and prevents loose clothing hazards

‚ö†Ô∏è Spring Compressor Safety

NEVER attempt spring work without proper equipment!

  • Always use manufacturer-approved spring compressors
  • Inspect compressor for damage before each use
  • Ensure hooks are fully engaged on spring coils
  • Compress evenly on both sides
  • Keep face and body away from spring ends
  • Wear face shield in addition to safety glasses

Legal & Organisational Requirements

Health & Safety at Work Act 1974

Key responsibilities:

  • Employers must provide safe working environment
  • Employees must follow safety procedures
  • Proper training must be provided
  • Risk assessments required for all tasks

Waste Disposal Regulations

Proper disposal procedures for:

  • Used shock absorbers: Drain fluid, dispose via registered waste carrier
  • Old springs: Scrap metal recycling
  • Bushings and rubber components: General waste or recycling
  • Hydraulic fluids: Hazardous waste - special disposal required

MOT Requirements

Suspension components are checked during MOT testing:

  • Excessive wear in bushings or ball joints = FAIL
  • Leaking shock absorbers = FAIL
  • Broken or damaged springs = FAIL
  • Loose or missing components = FAIL
  • Corrosion affecting structural integrity = FAIL

Routine Maintenance Tasks

1. Visual Inspection (Every Service)

  • Check all visible components for damage
  • Look for fluid leaks from shock absorbers
  • Inspect rubber boots and bushings
  • Check for corrosion on springs and mounts
  • Document findings with photos if needed

2. Bounce Test

  • Push down firmly on each corner of vehicle
  • Release and observe rebound
  • Good shock: One bounce, returns to rest
  • Failed shock: Multiple bounces or slow return
  • Test all four corners

3. Ball Joint Check

  • Raise vehicle and support safely
  • Grasp wheel at top and bottom
  • Try to rock wheel in and out
  • Excessive play indicates wear
  • Use pry bar to check specific joints

4. Bushing Inspection

  • Look for cracks, tears, or deformation
  • Check for separation from metal sleeves
  • Inspect for oil contamination
  • Feel for excessive movement
  • Compare left and right sides

‚ö†Ô∏è Measurement & Documentation

Always use proper measurement methods and document findings:

  • Use dial gauge for precise measurements
  • Record all measurements in service records
  • Compare to manufacturer specifications
  • Take photos of any damage or wear
  • Note vehicle mileage and date

Strut Removal Procedure (Overview)

Basic procedure for removing a MacPherson strut assembly:

Step-by-step process:

  1. Ensure vehicle is safely supported
  2. Remove wheel
  3. Disconnect brake hose/ABS sensor (if attached)
  4. Remove lower strut-to-hub mounting bolts
  5. Support strut from underneath
  6. Remove top mount nuts (under bonnet)
  7. Carefully lower strut assembly
  8. Use spring compressor to disassemble if needed
  9. Inspect all components
  10. Reverse procedure for installation
  11. Torque all fasteners to specification
  12. Check wheel alignment after installation
üî¥ CRITICAL: Never attempt to remove top mount nut without compressing spring first! The spring is under extreme tension and can cause serious injury.
